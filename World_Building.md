# World building

## 1. Human kingdoms

### 1.1 The Shards of Authority

Without a living God of Rules, humanity has turned "Order" into a weaponized bureaucracy.

#### 1.1.1 The Iron Ledger (The Holy City)
This isn't just a place of worship; it’s the world’s central bank and legal capital. The "God of Rules" has been reinterpreted as the Divine Auditor.

* The "Compliance" Tax: Citizens are taxed based on their "predictability." Those with irregular schedules or "adventurous" lifestyles pay more.

* The Emissaries: These aren't priests; they are high-ranking lawyers and adjudicators who resolve trade disputes with rigid, ancient law-books.

#### 1.1.2 The Merchant-States of the Spire
The Clockwork Spire is no longer a research facility; it's a factory.

* The Great Gears: Trade guilds fight for control over the "Logic-Chains" (ancient automated assembly lines) to produce high-end gear and "Boots" for the military.

* The Scholar Caste: These are essentially "Patent Attorneys" who hoard knowledge of the Spire’s mechanics to keep the monopoly on technology.

### 1.2 The Wild Fringes & Outcast Societies

Where the kingdoms see "Rules," the fringes see "Opportunity" (the bastardized remains of the God of Adventure’s influence).

#### 1.2.1 The Scavenger Economy (Reclaimed City)
Since the nobles gave up on this forest, it has become a "Gold Rush" town for the desperate.

* The Symbiote Slums: Lower-class workers who "bond" with forest organisms to survive the toxic spores, creating a new, shunned sub-species.

* The "Green-Market": A black market for magical reagents found in the overgrowth, sold to Alchemists under the table.

#### 1.2.2 The Pirate Coves (The Free-Trade Coalition)
This isn't just a bunch of thieves; it's a functional, if violent, society that thrives on bypassing "The Iron Ledger’s" taxes.

* Tidal Corsairs: They act as "unlicensed couriers," moving goods through labyrinthine tunnels that the official navy is too "orderly" to navigate.

## 2. The "Magic Problem" (History as a Hazard)

Magic isn't a gift; it's a contaminated resource from a war no one remembers.

The "Pagan" Ruins: People don't go here to pray; they go here to strip-mine.

The Malediction of Knowledge: In modern society, being a "Sorcerer" or "Bloodmage" is like being a nuclear physicist in a world that has banned electricity. You are useful for war, but citizens cross the street when they see you.

Chants as Codes: "Chants" are treated like lost encryption keys found in ruins, used to unlock ancient vaults or weaponize old statues.

## 4. Political structures

### 4.1. The Iron Ledger (The High Adjudicate)

In the Holy City, "Order" isn't a prayer; it’s a balance sheet. The government functions as a massive, world-spanning auditing firm.
The Political Hierarchy

The Grand Auditors: The ruling council who interprets the "Ancient Statutes" (heavily misinterpreted laws of the God of Rules). They believe that if the world can be perfectly quantified, it can be perfectly controlled.

Emissaries (The Enforcers): These are the field agents. They don't just "fight"; they "correct". In combat, an Emissary treats an enemy's existence as a clerical error to be deleted.

The Census-Takers: Low-level spies who monitor the "predictability" of citizens. If you take a different route to work three days in a row, your "Risk Premium" (tax rate) goes up.

### 4.2. The Pirate Coalition (The Gilded Wake)

The Coalition is the natural allergic reaction to the Iron Ledger. They exist in the "margins" of the Ledger—the places where the numbers don't add up.
#### 4.2.1 The Political Hierarchy

The Council of Coves: A loose group of Pirate Lords who realize that if they fight each other too much, the Ledger wins. Their "laws" are written in disappearing ink.

The "Unbound": Mercenaries, Thieves, and Bar Brawlers who have literally "scratched their names" out of the Ledger’s records.

Tidal Corsairs: The elite navigators of the Coalition, used to outmaneuver the rigid formations of the Ledger’s navy.

#### 4.2.2 Key Locations

The Ink-Stained Reef: A graveyard of Ledger ships where pirates "salvage" (steal) legal documents to forge new identities or property deeds.

The Maelstrom Market: A floating trade hub that moves every 24 hours to stay ahead of the Ledger’s "Predictability Models".

### 4.3. The Economic Cold War

The real conflict happens through the exchange of Fortune’s Favors.
| Feature | The Iron Ledger | The Pirate Coalition |
|---------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Primary Export | Certified Equipment & "Safe" Travel Permits | Forbidden | Reagents & "Lost" Artifacts |
View on Magic | A "Chaos Variable" that must be taxed or suppressed | A "Wild Card" used to tip the scales in a losing fight |
| Social Structure | Rigid Class Tiers (Rank/Level importance) | Meritocratic (If you can take it, it's yours) |

### 4.4. Points of Friction (Plot Hooks)

#### 4.4.1 The "Audit" Raids
The Ledger doesn't "invade" the Pirate Coves; they send a fleet to "perform a mandatory audit," which usually involves burning any ship that doesn't have a valid registration.

#### 4.4.2. The Shadow Debt
Many Pirate Lords actually owe massive amounts of "Fortune's Favor" to the Adventurer's Guilds inside Ledger cities, creating a weird, tense neutrality in the Hub Areas.

#### 4.4.3 The Fugitive Alchemist
An Alchemist from the Reclaimed City has fled to the Pirate Coves with a recipe for "False-Gold," a mineral that can fool the Ledger’s magical detection systems.

### 4.5. The Caught: Races in the Crossfire

These species are geographically or economically trapped by the human trade war.
#### 4.5.1 The Trolls: The "Biological Assets"

The Ledger’s View: The Iron Ledger classifies Trolls as "Heavy Machinery" rather than citizens, subjecting them to "Maintenance Taxes" instead of income taxes.

The Pirate’s View: Pirates see Trolls as the ultimate "Shield," often recruiting them from the Symbiote Slums with promises of "Supplies" that never quite cover their massive hunger.

Role in Conflict: Many Trolls work in the Clockwork Spire, providing the raw physical power to turn the massive gears that the scholars' "Logic Chains" cannot.

#### 4.5.2 The Harpies: The "High-Altitude Couriers"

The Trade Tension: Harpies control the air-currents between the Holy City and the Pirate Coves, making them the fastest way to move "Fortune’s Favors".

The Ledger’s Trap: The Ledger has implemented "Airspace Compliance Fees," forcing Harpies to fly in rigid, predictable patterns that make them easy targets for sky-pirates.

The Pirate’s Trap: Pirates hire them to drop "Burning Bolas" on Ledger supply lines, often leaving the Harpies to take the legal blame when things go wrong.

### 4.6 The Outsiders: Races Beyond the Ledger

These species view the human obsession with "Supplies" and "Area Unlocks" as a temporary madness.
#### 4.6.1 The Fae: The Geometric Architects

Philosophy: The Fae represent the "harsh growth" that reclaimed the Reclaimed City. They don't want gold; they want the city to return to a state of "symmetrical nature".

Relationship with Magic: While humans shun magic as a "Pagan" remnant, viewing human "Letters and Numbers" as a crude attempt to mimic the natural world.

Neutrality: They ignore the trade war unless a "Caravan" enters their forest without a "Scholar" to negotiate the specific "Chants" required for safe passage.

#### 4.6.2 The Centaurs: The Nomad Archivists

Philosophy: Closely tied to the God of Adventure’s Caravan, Centaurs value experiences and speed over permanent settlement.

The Long View: They believe the "Forgotten God" isn't a myth to be worshipped, but a set of puzzle mechanics to be solved through world exploration.

Neutrality: They possess knowledge of the ancient ruins that both the Ledger and Pirates want, but they refuse to trade it for currency, accepting only stories in exchange.

## 5. Locations

### 5.1. The Sub-Levels: The Under-Spire and the Sinking Sluices

If the Clockwork Spire is a gleaming factory of logic, what happens to the "exhaust"?

The Grease-Pits: A sprawling slum located literally beneath the Great Gears. The people here live in the shadows of massive pistons. They don't use clocks; they measure time by the rhythmic thrum of the machinery above.

The Filter-Folk: A new "species" or mutation—humans who have spent generations breathing in the metallic dust of the Spire. Their skin has a dull, brassy sheen, and they are obsessed with "Maintenance" as a religion, believing that if the gears stop, the world ends.

Mineral - Malfunction Quartz: A jagged, oily crystal that grows where machine oil meets magical runoff. It causes nearby mechanical objects to operate in reverse.

### 5.2. The Frozen Ledger: The Vaults of the North

The God of Rules had a "Backup" or an archive.

The Glacial Archives: A mountain range where the snow never melts because it isn't water—it’s tiny, frozen flakes of "Inert Information."

The Silent Monks of the Abacus: A faction that believes speech is too imprecise. They communicate via complex hand signals and bead-counting. They guard the "Original Statutes," which are carved into the tectonic plates of the earth.

Fauna - Ledger-Yeti: Massive, white-furred creatures that don't hunt for food, but for "Disorder." They are attracted to campfires and singing, seeking to extinguish anything that isn't silent and "regulated."

### 5.3. The Churning Marches: The God of Magic’s "Scar"

The betrayal of the third god isn't just a memory; it is a geographical wound.

The Glass Weald: A forest where the betrayal happened. The trees were turned to solid, colored glass mid-growth. The "Leaves" are razor-sharp and chime like bells in the wind.

The Refracted: People who stayed too long in the Weald. Their shadows no longer match their bodies; sometimes their shadows move a few seconds before they do.

Flora - Memory-Vine: A plant that grows by absorbing the "Recent History" of those nearby. If you stand near it, you might forget why you entered the forest, while the vine sprouts flowers that look like your childhood home.

### 5.4. Ruins of the forgotten god, god of magic

The "Strip-Mining" Conflict

Because magic is shunned but the reagents are valuable, a "Gold Rush" mentality has formed around these ruins.

* The Ledger’s Approach: They classify these ruins as "Hazardous Waste Sites" to justify seizing any artifacts found there as "Contraband".

* The Pirates’ Approach: They hire "unlicensed" mages (Bloodmages and Cultists) to stabilize the reality-warping effects just long enough to haul out the Gold-Thread Vines or Soot-Glass.

Note on Chants: In these ruins, "Chants" act as physical keys. Reciting the correct ancient phrase doesn't just cast a spell; it literally reshapes the ruins' geometry, opening doors that weren't there or calming the "Temporal Backflow".

#### 5.5.1. The Ossuary of Stolen Hues

Located deep within the Reclaimed City, this ruin is a crumbling cathedral where the very concept of "color" has become unstable.

The Phenomenon: Chromatic Bleed. Within the ruin, colors detach from objects. A person’s blue eyes might drift off their face and hover in the air, or the green of the moss might "stain" the air itself, leaving the ground a dull, powdery grey.

The Hazard: Visitors who stay too long become "Refracted"—their skin becomes translucent, and they begin to perceive sounds as tastes and light as physical weight.

The Loot: Scavengers hunt for Prism-Salt, a crystalline residue left behind when a color "dies" and turns into a physical mineral.

#### 5.5.2. The Vault of Recursive Seconds

A site often targeted by the Clockwork Spire’s scholars, this ruin is believed to be a former laboratory of the God of Magic.

The Phenomenon: Temporal Backflow. Time does not flow linearly here. A visitor might see their own "after-image" walking toward them from the future, or find their footprints already present on a dusty floor they haven't stepped on yet.

The Hazard: "Stutter-Crows" nest here, feasting on the fragmented memories of those who get caught in a ten-second time loop.

The Political Friction: The Iron Ledger attempts to "audit" the time inside, sending Emissaries to plant stationary mechanical clocks to "enforce" a standard second, though the clocks usually melt or turn into birds after a while.

## 6. Communities and groups

### 6.1. House Aethelgard: The Silent Creditors

To bridge the gap between the rigid bureaucracy of the Iron Ledger and the lawless discovery of the Pagan Ruins, we can introduce House Aethelgard, a noble lineage that thrives on the hypocrisy of the world's current order.

While publicly presenting themselves as the most devout practitioners of the God of Rules' doctrines, House Aethelgard operates as the premier "shadow bank" for expeditions into forbidden territories. They reside in the Holy City, where their manor is built with perfect mathematical symmetry to avoid "Predictability" taxes.

#### 6.1.1. The Public Face: The High Adjudicators

Members of the house often serve as Grand Auditors, overseeing the collection of "Compliance Taxes" from the common citizenry.

They are the loudest voices calling for the destruction of Pagan Ruins, claiming these sites are "clerical errors" in the fabric of reality that must be deleted.

They maintain a monopoly on "Safe Travel Permits," ensuring that only their authorized caravans can move between cities without facing heavy "Risk Premiums".

#### 6.1.2. The Private Reality: The "Black-Ink" Ledgers

The house maintains a second set of books, written in Echo-Glass dust, which records their investments in "unlicensed" salvage operations.

They secretly fund the Scavenger Economy in the Reclaimed City, providing the initial "Supplies" and "Fortune's Favors" needed for desperate adventurers to enter the forest.

Their goal is to recover Chants as Codes—ancient encrypted magic—to automate the Logic-Chains in the Clockwork Spire without needing to pay the Scholar Caste for their expertise.

#### 6.1.3. Key figures

| Name | Role | Motivation |
|------|------|------------|
| Matriarch Valerica | Grand Auditor | Believes that if she can "solve" the God of Magic’s imprisonment, she can tax reality itself. |
| Caspian Aethelgard | The "Audit-Raider" | A field agent who leads "Compliance Audits" on Pirate ships, specifically to "seize" (steal) magical reagents for the family. Often delegated onto lesser or more dangerous tasks and has such developed to resent the Matriach Valerica in hopes of usurping her. |
| The Ghost Scholar | Disgraced Advisor | A former member of the Clockwork Spire who was "deleted" from official records and now deciphers Pagan texts in the Aethelgard basement. |

#### 6.1.4. Points of Friction

* The "Laundered" Relics

House Aethelgard takes raw magical artifacts—like Gold-Thread Vines or Soot-Glass—and "refines" them in the Clockwork Spire. By the time the items hit the market, they are stamped with "Official Ledger Certification," making them legal for use by the wealthy while the poor are still arrested for possessing the raw materials.

* The Fae Watch

The Fae in the Reclaimed City have noticed that Aethelgard-funded expeditions always target Logic-Moss patches. The Fae have begun "growing" the forest into the House's secret outposts, slowly turning the stone walls back into living, chaotic wood.

* Messy origin

The house was originally a lesser house on the verge of extinction when they came across pirates and thieves to form a cooperative alliance in purpose to smuggle the artifacts to regain their stading. This leaves them in a dangerous position if found out and even though the pirates and thieves are paid accordingly they hold the houses secret as hostage and hold over them.

### 6.2. The Khasar Fleet: The Achivists of momentum

The Khasar Fleet is not a navy, but a massive, land-based "regime of hooves" that views the God of Adventure’s legacy not as a story, but as a map that must be constantly traversed to remain valid. While the Iron Ledger attempts to quantify the world through static numbers, the Fleet believes the world only exists where it is currently being felt by a thundering gallop.

#### 6.2.1. The Public Face: The "Wind-Post" Couriers

Members of the Fleet are the only entities fast enough to outrun the "glitching" Stutter-Crows or navigate the Temporal Leaks of the Churning Marches.

* The Sovereign Transit: They claim "Ancestral Right" to any path that has been walked by a Great Caravan for over three generations.

* The Story-Tax: Instead of gold, they often demand "New Experiences" from travelers—oral histories or secrets they don't yet possess—to allow passage through their migration cycles.

* Neutral Arbitrators: Because they value "Experience" and "Speed" over permanent settlement, they often act as neutral couriers for "Fortune’s Favors" between the Holy City and the Pirate Coves.

#### 6.2.2. The Private Reality: The "Hoof-Iron" Cartel

The Fleet maintains a secret economic grip on the world's logistics, ensuring that no "Caravan" can move safely without a Centaur navigator to interpret the environment.

* The Gear-Monopoly: They secretly trade "Soot-Glass" and "Gold-Thread Vines" harvested from forbidden ruins to the Clockwork Spire in exchange for specialized, high-grade alloys used for their "Hoof-Irons".

* The "Stagnation" Sabotage: To prevent the Iron Ledger from building permanent roads through the plains, the Fleet’s Rootless Tribes will often "re-route" Logic-Moss into construction sites, causing the Ledger’s infrastructure to be reclaimed by the forest.

* The Memory-Blackmail: Their "Vocal-Archive" contains the true names and "Shadow Debts" of prominent Ledger Auditors, which they use as leverage to avoid being taxed for their nomadic lifestyle.

#### 6.2.4. Points of Friction

* The "Audit" of the Open Road

The Iron Ledger has attempted to implement "Airspace and Ground Compliance Fees" for Centaur migration paths. In response, the Fleet has begun delivering "Compliance Alerts" to the wrong cities, causing massive bureaucratic chaos within the Ledger's legal capital.

* The Refracted Scouts

Many Centaur outriders who spend too much time in the "Glass Weald" or near "Temporal Leaks" have their shadows detached. These "Refracted" Centaurs are shunned by the Great Caravans, often fleeing to the Pirate Coves to serve as "unbound" mercenaries.

* The Bronze-Skin Tensions

The "Filter-Folk" beneath the Clockwork Spire have started stealing "Hoof-Iron" from Centaur camps to use in their machine maintenance. This has led to "Audit Raids" where Centaur Regimes trample the "Grease-Pits" in retaliation.


## 7. The Centaurs

### 7.1. The Centaurian "Thundering" Regimes

Centaur society is not governed by land ownership, but by The Pace. They are split into three primary organizational structures:

#### 7.1.1 The Great Caravans (The Moving Villages)

These are massive, rolling settlements consisting of reinforced wagons pulled by the Centaurs themselves.

* The Structure: These aren't just groups of travelers; they are mobile fortifications. The wagons are tiered—the inner circle houses the elderly and the young, while the outer rim consists of "Scout-Sleeper" wagons that can be detached in seconds.

* The Law of the Road: If a wagon breaks an axle and cannot be repaired before the sun sets, it is stripped and burned. To stay still is to invite "Stagnation," which they view as a spiritual sickness.

#### 7.1.2 The Regimes (The Warrior-Administrators)

A "Regime" is a high-speed military and political unit. Unlike the Iron Ledger’s lawyers, a Regime’s leader (the Khasar) makes decisions based on the current wind and terrain.

* The Doctrine of Momentum: Their "laws" are based on the path ahead. If a territory is fertile, the law is "Harvest and Move." If it is hostile, the law is "Strike and Fade."

* Impact on Others: They often act as "Toll-Keepers" of the open plains. They don't want to conquer your city, but they will charge you a "Safe Passage" fee in the form of stories or unique salts to let your merchant caravans cross their migration paths.

#### 7.1.3 The Rootless Tribes (The Outriders)

Smaller, faster groups that refuse the safety of the Great Caravans. They are the "Archive-Hunters" who seek out the most dangerous ruins. They value the Map of the Mind more than any physical object.

### 7.2. Values and Treasures

Centaurs have a unique relationship with physical objects because they cannot carry much.

* Memory-Tapestries: Since they don't use paper (too fragile for the road), they weave their history into their coats or specialized silken sashes. A Centaur’s rank can be read by the complexity of the embroidery on their flank-wraps.

* The "Star-Glass" Compass: They treasure ancient navigation tools that align with the constellations rather than magnetic north. They believe the God of Adventure left "veins of luck" across the world that can only be found by following specific star-paths.

* Hoof-Iron: They don't use standard currency. Instead, they trade in high-grade, enchanted horse-shoes. Each set is etched with a "Travelogue"—a record of where those shoes have walked. A set of shoes that has crossed the Glass Weald is worth more than a chest of gold to a Centaur.

### 7.3. Survival and Global Impact

Centaurs survive by being the world's most elite Information Brokers.

* The "Wind-Post": Because they are the only race fast enough to outrun the Stutter-Crows and navigate the Churning Marches, they act as the unofficial postal service of the world. Even the Iron Ledger begrudgingly pays them to deliver high-priority "Compliance Alerts" across long distances.

* Ecological Impact: Their migration cycles are so predictable that other races have built "Waystation Markets" along their routes. A city’s economy can flourish or die based on whether a Great Caravan decides to "graze" near their walls for a season.

* The "Vocal-Archive": They are the keepers of oral history. While the Ledger hoards books, the Centaurs hoard names. They know the true names of the Pirate Lords’ grandfathers and the hidden locations of the Sinking Sluices, which they only trade for "New Experiences"—essentially, information they don't already possess.
